# Explanation — Writing Guide

> Source: [diataxis.fr/explanation](https://diataxis.fr/explanation/)

Explanation is a discursive treatment of a subject that permits reflection. It is
understanding-oriented — it deepens and broadens comprehension by bringing clarity,
light and context.

## Key Characteristics

- Takes a higher and wider perspective than other documentation types
- Scope bounded by a topic ("an area of knowledge"), not a task or feature
- Answers the question: "Can you tell me about...?"
- The only documentation type that might make sense to read away from the keyboard
- Not a luxury — equally important as other types, despite feeling less urgent

## Writing Principles

### 1. Make Connections

Connect the subject to other things, even those outside the immediate topic. The goal is
to weave a web of understanding that places concepts in relation to each other.

### 2. Provide Context

Include background: why things exist (design decisions, historical reasons, technical
constraints). Draw implications. Mention specific examples that illuminate the concept.

### 3. Talk "About" the Subject

Effective discussion areas include:

- The bigger picture
- History and evolution
- Choices and trade-offs
- Alternatives and possibilities
- Reasons and justifications

**Naming test:** Titles should allow an implicit "About" prefix. If "About [title]"
reads naturally, the title is well-scoped.

### 4. Admit Opinion and Perspective

Consider alternatives, counter-examples, and multiple approaches. Position explanation
as discussion that can weigh contrary opinions — not as a single authoritative decree.

### 5. Keep Explanation Closely Bounded

**Critical anti-pattern:** Explanation tends to absorb other content types — instruction,
technical description, how-to steps. This dilutes both the explanation and the material
that should live elsewhere. Explanation must resist becoming a dumping ground.

## Language Conventions

- "The reason for x is because historically, y..."
- "W is better than z, because..."
- "An x in system y is analogous to a w in system z. However..."
- "Some users prefer w (because z). This can be a good approach, but..."

## Alternative Names

Projects may use different labels for explanation content:

- Discussion
- Background
- Conceptual guides
- Topics

## Analogy

Harold McGee's *On Food and Cooking* (1984) — it does not teach cooking or contain recipes.
It places food in context of history, society, science, and technology. You read it for
reflection, not while cooking. It changes your understanding and indirectly affects your practice.
