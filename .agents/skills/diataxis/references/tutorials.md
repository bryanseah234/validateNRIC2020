# Tutorials — Writing Guide

> Source: [diataxis.fr/tutorials](https://diataxis.fr/tutorials/)

A tutorial is an experience that takes place under the guidance of a tutor — always
learning-oriented. The learner acquires skills and knowledge through doing something
meaningful toward an achievable goal.

## The Teacher-Student Contract

The teacher bears nearly all responsibility. The pupil's only job is to be attentive and
follow directions. Every exercise must be meaningful, successful, logical, and usefully complete.

## Critical Distinction

While a student learns by doing, what they **do** is not necessarily what they **learn**.
Through action, students acquire theoretical knowledge, understanding, familiarity with tools,
conceptual relationships, workflows, and terminology.

## Pedagogical Principles

### 1. Don't Try to Teach

Provide an experience that allows learning to happen. Trust that learning will take place
through the activity itself.

### 2. Show the Destination Upfront

Let the learner form an idea of what they will achieve from the start.

- **Good:** "In this tutorial we will create and deploy a scalable web application."
- **Bad:** "In this tutorial you will learn..." (presumptuous about what they will learn)

### 3. Deliver Visible Results Early and Often

Every step should produce a comprehensible result, however small. The learner needs
continuous confirmation that things are working.

### 4. Maintain a Narrative of Expected Outcomes

At each step, users experience anxiety about whether they are on the right path. Address this:

- "You will notice that..."
- "After a few moments, the server responds with..."
- Show example output
- Flag warning signs the learner might encounter

### 5. Point Out What to Notice

Learners focused on following steps miss environmental cues unless prompted. Close learning
loops by pointing things out in passing — help them see what matters beyond the immediate task.

### 6. Target the Feeling of Doing

Accomplished practice has a joined-up purpose, action, thinking and result that develops into
a confident rhythm. That feeling is what the tutorial should cultivate.

### 7. Encourage Repetition

Learners return to exercises that give them success. Repetition is sometimes the only teacher.

### 8. Ruthlessly Minimise Explanation

A tutorial is not the place for explanation. When following directions, the user is focused
on doing, not understanding. Provide links to extended discussion but never obstruct the flow.

### 9. Focus on the Concrete

Direct learners from step to concrete step. All learning moves from the concrete and particular
toward the general and abstract — not the other way around.

### 10. Ignore Options and Alternatives

Guidance must remain focused on what is required to reach the conclusion. Interesting diversions
can be left for another time.

### 11. Aspire to Perfect Reliability

A tutorial must inspire confidence. A learner who follows directions and does not get expected
results quickly loses confidence in the guide and in themselves.

## Anti-Pedagogical Temptations

Resist these when writing tutorials: **abstraction, generalisation, explanation, choices, information.**
Each pulls the reader out of the doing mode that tutorials depend on.

## Language Conventions

Use first-person plural ("We...") to create a sense of shared journey:

- "In this tutorial, we will..."
- "First, do x. Now, do y."
- "The output should look something like..."
- "Notice that..."
- "You have built a secure, three-layer hylomorphic stasis engine..."

## Maintenance Warning

Tutorials consume remarkable maintenance effort because product evolution cascades changes through
the entire narrative. Every step depends on prior steps — a single breakage undermines the whole experience.

## Analogy

Teaching a child to cook. Success is measured by the knowledge and skills the child acquires,
not by the quality of the food produced.
